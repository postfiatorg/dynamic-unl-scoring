# Scoring Prompt v8 — Advisory Overall Score

`prompts/scoring_v8.txt` replaces `scoring_v7.txt` as the active scoring prompt, shipping together with score formula v1 (`docs/DeterministicFinalScore.md`): the deterministic formula now owns the authoritative final score, so the prompt stops demanding from the model the one thing two controlled replays proved it cannot deliver — global cross-validator consistency of the overall score. `scoring_v7.txt` is retained on disk for audit.

## The change

Five edits against v7, all in service of one reframing; the response schema is unchanged:

1. The two-step scoring protocol (fix sub-scores, then derive every overall score as one uniform function of them) is replaced by an honest statement: the overall score is the model's holistic advisory judgment, and the network computes the authoritative final score from the five sub-scores with a fixed, published deterministic formula. The sub-scores carry the model's entire effective judgment.
2. The dominance self-check rule ("check overall scores for dominance consistency before answering") is removed — the formula guarantees this property by construction.
3. The overall-score description in the output instructions ("representing your final judgment") becomes "representing your holistic advisory judgment".
4. The user-prompt output list asks for "an advisory overall score" instead of "an overall score".
5. The response-schema note for the `score` field reads "advisory overall score".

Everything else stays: the full-resolution consensus rule, the recent-windows-dominate rule, the per-dimension evidence-ordering rules, the diversity concentration ordering, the exact `validator_id` output rule, and both few-shot examples. These are the v7 additions the replays showed the model applies reliably, and they remain the contract for the sub-scores the formula consumes.

The `score` field stays in the response schema deliberately: deployed validator sidecars' vendored parser requires it, and removing it would break their LLM-level verification (compatibility invariant 1 in `docs/DeterministicFinalScore.md`). The advisory score is still published in `outputs/validator_scores.json`, preserving a permanent measurement of how far the model's holistic judgment drifts from the formula.

## Validation

Round 15's frozen `model_request.json` was re-rendered through the v8 template with validator data verified byte-identical to the frozen round (only the template text differing), and replayed on the round's pinned runtime (model revision `e89b16eb…`, SGLang image digest `5d9ec715…`, H100, temperature 0). The formula and the round's real selection parameters (cutoff 40, max size 20, min gap 5, churn against round 14's UNL) were then applied to the replay output, exercising the full v8 + formula pipeline end to end. Results, compared against the published round and the v7 replay recorded in `ScoringPromptV7.md`:

- **Clean parse and stable sub-scores.** All 50 validators parsed with exact IDs and no errors. Against the v7 replay, reliability and software sub-scores are identical for all 50 validators, consensus for 49 of 50 (one validator moved 97 → 98 — a single point of full-resolution judgment noise), diversity for 45, and identity for 38. Every change is class-uniform: the identity moves are one band shift (50 → 45) applied to all twelve validators without a verified domain that held 50, and the diversity move is one whole group (below). No within-group split appeared anywhere.
- **The formula layer is exactly consistent.** On the replay output: zero identical-sub-score groups with divergent final scores and zero dominance violations — the properties hold empirically, not just by construction. The round 15 anomaly is dead: all six members of the former 92-versus-88 group receive final score 90, including the original rank-1 outlier (its advisory score still shows the +4 artifact — 92 against its twin's 92 and the others' 88 — which now affects nothing).
- **Offline validators are ineligible with margin.** The three validators with 0 % 1-hour agreement score consensus 0 and final 25 (advisory 15–25). Advisory-versus-final drift across the set: mean 4.5 points, maximum 22 (at the degraded tail, where the model's holistic severity judgment and the formula's gate arrive at similar conclusions by different routes).
- **Selection matches the round's boundary behavior.** 20 seats, no eligibility-cutoff crossings, 19/20 overlap with the published UNL — the single difference is the same churn-control seat the v7 replay produced (`pft.whiteguy.eu` retained over `pft.g.money`), legitimate score movement rather than the anomaly class.
- **The leaderboard reorders on evidence, not artifacts.** The published rank-1 validator (`jollydinger.com`) moves to the middle of its identical-evidence cohort (#1 → #20, final 90) — where its data always placed it. The new top cohort is a seven-way tie at 93 combining perfect consensus with high diversity, so diversity now genuinely differentiates equally-reliable validators. Validators whose holistic model score carried punishment their own sub-scores never expressed rise accordingly (`pft.zomdeifel.net`, #45 → #22: advisory 65 against strong sub-scores — exactly the hidden-judgment class the formula eliminates). Scores compress into ties (for example an eleven-validator band at 90): honest behavior, since the former one-point distinctions between identical evidence were artifacts, and ties break deterministically by master key.
- **Residual known imperfection, documented deliberately.** The five-validator Finland/Hetzner group's diversity rose 75 → 85 in this replay, tying Singapore/Cherry and Japan/Vultr — a cross-validator diversity-ordering wrinkle of the same class the v7 replay showed (there, Japan tied Singapore while Finland sat correctly below). Comparative sub-score judgment across validators remains the model's noisiest surface; it moved one final score by a single point with no selection impact, and the formula cannot repair sub-score-level noise by design — that layer deliberately stays the model's judgment, verified by sidecars at the PARSED level.
