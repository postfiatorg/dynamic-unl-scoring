# Scoring Prompt v7 — Consistency and Evidence-Ordering Revision

`prompts/scoring_v7.txt` replaces `scoring_v6.txt` as the active scoring prompt. It adds explicit consistency and evidence-ordering rules to the scoring contract so that equal evidence produces equal scores and better evidence produces better scores. No selection threshold, dimension weighting philosophy, response schema, or operating parameter changed; `scoring_v6.txt` is retained on disk for audit.

The revision succeeded at the sub-score level and failed at the overall-score level: replays show the model applies per-dimension rules reliably but cannot hold a global cross-validator consistency constraint on the overall score. The overall-score fix is deferred to a follow-up that computes the overall score deterministically from the five sub-scores (see Outcome below). The consistency rules remain in the template as the stated contract; the replay evidence below documents how far the model actually satisfies them.

## Why

A community validator operator questioned testnet round 15's rankings on the public scoring UI. A comparison of the published artifacts for testnet rounds 12–15 (frozen input packages against `outputs/validator_scores.json`) confirmed a real inconsistency class in the model's judgment, appearing in every round examined:

- **Identical evidence, different overall score.** Round 15's rank-1 validator scored 92 while a validator with identical scoring evidence — every field the model sees equal except the domain name string — scored 88. The 4-point artifact alone produced the rank-1 position. Three further validators differing only in their 30-day missed-ledger counts also scored 88 with an identical sub-score vector.
- **Identical sub-score vectors, divergent overall scores.** Every round examined has at least one group of validators sharing all five dimensional sub-scores yet diverging in overall score: three groups in round 12, two in round 13, one in round 14, and three in round 15 (spreads of 1–4 points in the selected range, 10 points at the bottom of the table). Outside these groups, overall scores tracked the sub-scores coherently — round 15's identical-sub-score cohort stepped monotonically with diversity (80 → 92, 75 → 91, 65 → 91, 55 → 90, 50 → 88, 40 → 88) with the rank-1 outlier as the single exception.
- **Ordering violations within a dimension.** A validator with 22 missed ledgers in the 30-day window received reliability 85 while validators with 25–6,148 missed ledgers received reliability 90. In the diversity dimension, the set's only Singapore validator on its only Cherry Servers endpoint tied (75) the five-validator Finland group hosted on the set's most concentrated provider ASN, while a Japan validator on the second-most concentrated provider received 80. Round 15's eleven-validator Germany/Hetzner group also split 55/60 with no evidence difference.

The dimensional sub-scores were otherwise the consistent part of the output: the model banded them (93–100 % of sub-score values across rounds 12–15 are multiples of 5 — an emergent habit, nothing in the parser or prompt requires it) while emitting fine-grained overall scores. The noise therefore lived in the final holistic step, precisely where the prompt imposed no consistency constraint.

The round 15 anomaly had no selection impact — with the outlier corrected to its evidence-twin's 88, the same 20 validators clear selection (regression-tested in `tests/test_unl_selector.py`) — but score gaps at the selection boundary in rounds 12–15 were 0–2 points, smaller than the observed noise, so the class could flip a marginal seat in a future round. It also makes the public leaderboard's ordering partly artifactual, which is what the community noticed.

## The change

Four rule classes were added to the prompt template (classes 1–3 in the system-prompt section, class 4 in the user-prompt section), plus supporting few-shot edits:

1. **Uniform overall scoring.** Scoring is framed as two steps: fix each validator's five sub-scores from its evidence, then derive every overall score from the sub-scores alone. The overall score remains the model's own weighted judgment (not a mechanical average), but the weighting must be one consistent function applied to the whole round: identical sub-score vectors must receive identical overall scores, any overall difference must be traceable to a sub-score difference, and a validator whose sub-scores are all greater than or equal to another's must not receive a lower overall score (dominance check).
2. **Full-resolution consensus scoring.** The consensus sub-score must reflect the numeric agreement evidence without banding to multiples of 5: identical agreement evidence across the three windows scores identically, and strictly better evidence (no window worse, at least one better) scores strictly higher. The three windows are not interchangeable — the recent windows dominate, so a validator with near-zero 1-hour or 24-hour agreement must be penalized heavily in the consensus sub-score itself, never only in the overall score. Consensus is the one dimension with continuous numeric evidence; the remaining dimensions rest on discrete evidence (version strings, verification booleans, concentration situations) where finer numbers would be false precision, so no granularity requirement was added there.
3. **Per-dimension evidence ordering.** Within every dimension, identical evidence must produce identical sub-scores and strictly better evidence a strictly better sub-score. For diversity this is made concrete as concentration ordering: a validator no more concentrated than another on either axis (country or ASN) and strictly less concentrated on at least one must score strictly higher, and equivalent concentration situations must score identically.
4. **Exact output identifiers.** The response must copy each `validator_id` exactly as given, including the leading `v` (added after replay 1, where the model emitted `008` for `v008` and the round would have failed on the incomplete parse — see Revision history).

The two few-shot `reasoning` examples now ground their consensus sub-scores in explicit missed-ledger evidence (a flawless three-window record versus a 30-day tail keeping consensus at 96), demonstrating full-resolution consensus scoring; their countries and providers remain ones absent from the live sets, as established by v6.

No post-inference score mutation was introduced: scoring judgment stays entirely inside the model, and the parser, selector, artifact structure, and execution-manifest schema are unchanged. The template is hash-pinned in each round's execution manifest (`code.prompt.template_sha256`, recomputed from the active template file and regression-tested in `tests/test_ipfs_publisher.py`), so validator sidecars reproduce v7 rounds byte-identically with no sidecar-side changes.

## Revision history

The template landed in two revisions, both validated by replaying round 15 (see Validation):

- **Revision 1** carried rule classes 1–3 with a strict ("less concentrated on both axes") diversity ordering. Its replay surfaced two regressions. First, the model emitted the key `008` instead of `v008`, dropping one validator from the parsed result (49/50, parse incomplete) — in production the orchestrator fails the round on an incomplete parse, so this input shape would have failed every round deterministically. Second, the full-resolution consensus rule backfired for offline validators: a validator with 0 % 1-hour agreement received a consensus sub-score of ~99 read off its 30-day history, with the not-participating penalty displaced into the overall score — exactly the hidden holistic judgment v7 outlaws — producing identical-sub-score groups diverging by up to 49 points. Revision 1's raw model output was not preserved; these observations are recorded from the campaign session's analysis.
- **Revision 2** (the shipped template) added the two-step scoring frame, the recent-windows-dominate consensus rule, the exact-identifier rule, and weakened the diversity ordering to weak dominance (no more concentrated on either axis, strictly less on at least one). Its replay parsed cleanly and fixed both regressions.

## Validation

Round 15's frozen `model_request.json` was re-rendered through the v6 template and reproduced the round's messages byte-identically (system and user prompts exact, all 50 validators), confirming the re-render harness before the template swap. Both replays then ran on the round's pinned runtime (model revision `e89b16eb…`, SGLang image digest `5d9ec715…`, H100, temperature 0) with validator data verified byte-identical to the frozen round and only the template text differing; selection used the round's real parameters (cutoff 40, max size 20, min gap 5, churn against round 14's UNL).

Revision 2 replay versus the published round 15 outputs:

- **Sub-score rules hold.** All 50 validators parsed with exact IDs. The three degraded validators (0 % 1-hour agreement) receive consensus **0** in the sub-score itself (published: 10), with overall scores staying in the 15–25 band. Consensus de-bands where evidence is imperfect (values such as 83 and 97–99 appear; flawless validators remain at 100). Diversity is ordered by concentration and uniform within equivalent situations: Singapore/Cherry rises 75 → 85 above Finland/Hetzner (75), Germany/Hetzner unifies its published 55/60 split at 65, US/Vultr moves 40 → 50, unresolved endpoints 30 → 40. One residual ordering violation remains: the Japan validator on the shared Vultr ASN ties Singapore/Cherry at 85 despite Singapore's strictly less concentrated situation.
- **The overall-score consistency rules do not hold.** Five identical-sub-score groups diverge in overall score (published round: three), and the rank-1 artifact recurs in the same shape: two validators score 92 while four validators with the identical sub-score vector score 88. Weak-dominance violations between sub-score vectors rise from 19 ordered pairs (published) to 70 — the counts are not directly comparable because finer consensus and diversity resolution creates more comparable pairs, but at minimum the rules produced no improvement where they were aimed.
- **Selection impact stays at the boundary.** No validator crosses the score-40 eligibility line. The selected UNL differs from the published round by one churn-control seat: the incumbent `pft.whiteguy.eu` rises 82 → 85 on its diversity gain, lifting the displacement bar (weakest incumbent + 5) to 90, so the challenger `pft.g.money` (88 in both runs) no longer displaces it. This is legitimate score movement interacting with churn control, not the anomaly class.

## Outcome

v7 fixes what the community flagged at the dimension level — diversity concentration ordering, consensus tied to the numeric evidence, offline validators penalized where the penalty belongs — and hardens the output contract. It does not deliver overall-score consistency: two controlled replays show the model applies local, per-validator-checkable rules reliably but cannot enforce a global constraint across 50 validators in a single pass; the noise relocates instead of disappearing.

The overall-score fix is therefore planned as a separate change: the overall score becomes a deterministic, published function of the five dimensional sub-scores, computed in open-source code and content-hash-pinned in each round's execution manifest, with the model's judgment continuing to own the sub-scores. Until that lands, deployed rounds stay on the current production prompt; v7's replay behavior above is the baseline the follow-up will be measured against.
